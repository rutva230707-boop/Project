Understanding Anxiety

Gentle information and tools to help when worry feels too big.

What is anxiety?

Anxiety is a feeling of worry, fear, or unease that can show up in your body and your thoughts. It becomes a problem when it is very strong, happens often, or makes daily life hard.

Common symptoms

You may notice some of these signs when you feel anxious:

  • Feeling nervous, on edge, or like something bad might happen
  • Racing heart, faster breathing, sweating, or shaking
  • Tight muscles, headaches, or stomach discomfort
  • Difficulty concentrating or sleeping
  • Wanting to avoid people, places, or situations that make you anxious

Simple coping strategies

  • Slow breathing: Follow the 4–6–8 breathing from the home page to calm your body.
  • Grounding: Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste.
  • Gentle movement: Stretch, walk slowly, or move your body in a way that feels safe.
  • Talk to someone you trust: A friend, family member, teacher, or counsellor.
  • Keep a regular sleep routine and limit caffeine, especially later in the day.
1. Stay with you?

Do you want me (or someone you trust) to stay with you while this is happening?



2. Breathe together?

Can we try breathing slowly together using the 4–6–8 pattern?



3. Start grounding?

Can you name 5 things you can see around you right now?



4. Continue grounding?

Can you try 4 things you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, and 1 you can taste?



5. Make it easier?

Would anything make this feel a bit easier, like sitting down or holding something?



6. Talk or quiet?

Would you like me to talk gently, or just be quiet and sit with you?



7. Plan for next time?

When this calms down, would you like to decide together what helps you most next time?



When to reach out for help

Consider talking to a mental health professional or doctor if anxiety is stopping you from going to school, work, or doing daily activities, or if it has been very strong for several weeks.

This website offers general information and coping ideas. It is not a replacement for professional diagnosis, treatment, or emergency support. If you ever feel in danger of harming yourself or others, please contact local emergency services or a crisis helpline in your area.
