What are personality disorders?

Personality disorders are long‑term patterns in how a person thinks, feels, and relates to others that can cause distress or problems in daily life.

They are real mental health conditions, and support, therapy, and understanding can help people manage symptoms and build a life that fits their values.

Common challenges

  • Intense emotions that may change quickly or feel hard to control
  • Very strong fears of rejection, abandonment, or being hurt
  • Conflict in relationships or feeling misunderstood by others
  • Feeling unsure about identity, values, or goals
  • Urges to act quickly or in risky ways when distressed

Helpful approaches

  • Therapy: Talking therapies like DBT or other structured approaches can help with emotions and relationships.
  • Education: Learning about the diagnosis can reduce shame and increase self‑understanding.
  • Support networks: Safe friends, family, or peer groups can offer connection and validation.
  • Self‑care: Regular sleep, balanced food, movement, and calming activities support emotional regulation.

Questions for moments of strong emotion

1. Name the feeling

Can you pause for a moment and name one feeling you notice right now (for example: angry, sad, scared, empty)?



2. Check safety

Right now, are you physically safe in this moment (for example: in a room, not in immediate danger)?



3. Relationship pause

Before reacting to someone, can you take a short pause (for example: 5 slow breaths) before sending a message or speaking?



4. Support person

Is there someone safe you can tell, “My feelings are really big right now, can you listen for a few minutes?”



5. Plan with therapist

Do you have (or would you like to have) a plan with a therapist or professional for what to do when emotions feel overwhelming?



When to reach out for help

If intense emotions, relationship conflict, or impulsive actions are making life feel unmanageable, support from a mental health professional can help.

If you ever feel in danger of harming yourself or others, please contact emergency services or a crisis helpline right away. You deserve to stay safe while you get support.
