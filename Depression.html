Understanding Depression

Gentle information and small steps for heavy feelings.

What is depression?

Depression is a mood condition where feelings of sadness, emptiness, or lack of interest last for weeks or longer and affect daily life.

It is not a weakness. Many people experience depression, and with support and treatment, it can improve.

Common symptoms

You may notice some of these signs:

  • Feeling sad, empty, numb, or hopeless most of the day
  • Losing interest in activities you used to enjoy
  • Changes in sleep (sleeping too much or too little)
  • Low energy, feeling tired almost every day
  • Changes in appetite or weight
  • Feeling guilty, worthless, or very critical of yourself
  • Difficulty concentrating or making decisions

Simple coping strategies

  • Very small steps: Choose one tiny task, like brushing your teeth or drinking a glass of water.
  • Light movement: A short walk, gentle stretching, or simple exercises can slowly lift mood.
  • Routine: Try to wake up, eat, and sleep at similar times each day.
  • Connect: Talk to someone you trust, even if it is just a short message.
  • Limit alcohol and drugs, which can make depression worse.]

Check in with yourself

1. Tiny action

Can you do one tiny helpful action in the next 5 minutes (for example: drink water, open a window, or stand up and stretch)?



2. Talk to someone

Is there someone you could send a short message to, just to say “hi” or “I am having a hard time”?



3. Rest and sleep

Would a short rest, turning off screens, or going to bed at a regular time help your body tonight?



4. Enjoyable moment

Is there one small thing that usually brings you a little comfort (music, warm drink, sunlight, favourite show)?



5. Professional support

Would you consider talking with a doctor, counsellor, or mental health professional about how you are feeling?



When to reach out for help

If depression lasts most days for more than two weeks or makes it hard to work, study, or care for yourself, it is important to reach out for help.

If you ever think about harming yourself, please contact emergency services or a crisis helpline in your area immediately. You deserve support and safety.
